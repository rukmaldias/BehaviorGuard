# FAQ

---

## Privacy and data

### Does BehaviorGuard send any data to a server?

No. BehaviorGuard has no network calls, no analytics, and no telemetry of any kind. All signal collection, feature extraction, and model training happen entirely on-device. The only value that can leave the device is the `Float` risk score returned by `endSession()` — your app receives that and decides what to do with it.

### What exactly is stored on the device?

After enrollment, two encrypted blobs are written to `SharedPreferences`:

1. **Baseline profile** — 64 numbers (mean and standard deviation for each of the 32 features). These describe your statistical interaction pattern but cannot be reversed into your actual keystrokes or words typed.
2. **Autoencoder weights** — ~1,352 numbers representing the neural network. These encode correlations between features, not raw events.

Raw keystrokes, touch coordinates, and motion samples are held in memory only for the duration of a session and discarded immediately after the feature vector is extracted.

### Can the stored profile be used to recover what a user typed?

No. The baseline profile stores statistical aggregates (mean dwell time, mean pressure, etc.) — not individual events, words, or content. There is no way to reverse a mean value into the original keystrokes that produced it.

### What encryption protects the stored data?

AES-256-GCM with a 12-byte random nonce per write. The encryption key is 32 bytes generated by `SecureRandom`, stored in `EncryptedSharedPreferences` backed by Android Keystore. On devices with a hardware security module, the key never leaves secure hardware.

## Enrollment

### What happens if a session during enrollment is very short?

Short sessions (fewer than 5 keystrokes and fewer than 3 touches) return `SessionOutcome.Error` and are ignored — they do not count toward the 5-session enrollment requirement. The enrollment counter stays the same. This protects the baseline from being polluted by low-signal sessions.

### Does enrollment need to happen all at once?

No. Enrollment progress persists across app restarts. If a user completes 2 sessions today and 3 sessions next week, their 5th session still completes enrollment normally.

### Can I change the number of enrollment sessions required?

Not in the current API — 5 sessions is fixed in the Rust core. This value was chosen to balance baseline quality against the time cost of enrollment. Fewer sessions produce a noisier baseline and lower Phase 2 accuracy; more sessions delay when scoring becomes available.

### What if the user's behaviour during enrollment was unusual?

The baseline is built from whatever sessions you feed it. If a user enrolled while sick, one-handed, or on an unusually smooth surface, their baseline will reflect that. A noisy baseline produces a higher false-positive rate for the same user once they return to normal behaviour. Consider adding a "Re-enroll" option in your app's settings.

## Scoring

### Why am I getting a high risk score for myself right after enrollment?

A few possible causes:

1. **The enrolled sessions were not representative.** If your 5 enrollment sessions happened in unusual conditions (commuting, noisy environment, one-handed), the baseline doesn't reflect your normal behaviour.
2. **Not enough events per session.** A session with only 5 keystrokes produces a very limited feature vector. Aim for sessions with 20+ keystrokes for a stable baseline.
3. **The autoencoder hasn't fully converged.** With only 5 training samples (even augmented to 405), the autoencoder may not generalise perfectly. Scores typically stabilise after a few scoring sessions as you become more familiar with the flow.

Try calling `reset()` and re-enrolling under normal, comfortable conditions.

### What does a risk score of 0.0 mean?

It means the session's feature vector was reconstructed by the autoencoder with near-zero error — the behaviour exactly matches the trained pattern. In practice, scores below 0.1 are very typical for enrolled users interacting normally.

### The score fluctuates a lot between sessions. Is that normal?

Some fluctuation is expected — your typing rhythm changes slightly based on focus level, fatigue, and the content you're typing. The `confidence` field helps: a score of 0.5 with confidence 0.05 (very short session) is much less meaningful than a score of 0.5 with confidence 0.8 (rich, long session).

If you see large swings (e.g. 0.1 to 0.8) even in long sessions, consider whether:
- The interaction flows your app sends to BehaviorGuard are sufficiently varied
- The content of the interaction is consistent enough to produce comparable feature vectors

### Does the model update over time to track gradual behaviour change?

Not automatically in v0.1.0. The autoencoder is trained once at enrollment and does not update. Gradual natural drift (typing faster as you get more comfortable with a new phone, for example) can cause false positives over time. The recommended mitigation is to provide a voluntary re-enrollment option after 3–6 months.

## Device and lifecycle

### What happens if the user uninstalls and reinstalls the app?

The encrypted blobs in `SharedPreferences` are deleted with the app. The Keystore key is also deleted on uninstall (Android clears app-bound Keystore entries). The user starts from scratch and must re-enroll. This is the correct behaviour — a reinstall should not be able to bypass the re-enrollment requirement.

### What if the user changes devices?

The profile and model are tied to the Keystore key on the original device and cannot be transferred. On a new device, the user must re-enroll. This is intentional — the model is partly measuring device-specific interaction patterns (screen size, touch sensitivity, gyroscope calibration), not just the person in isolation.

### What happens if the user gets injured (broken arm, cuts on fingers)?

A significant physical change to the hand or arm will cause the enrolled baseline to no longer match the user's current behaviour, resulting in elevated risk scores. This is a fundamental limitation of behavioral biometrics. Provide a visible re-enrollment path and communicate clearly to users that they can re-enroll if their behaviour changes for any reason.

### Does it work on emulators?

Partially. Emulators have no physical gyroscope or accelerometer, so IMU features (features 18–31) will be zero for every session. Keystroke and touch features still work. This means:
- Enrollment and scoring will function
- Accuracy will be reduced compared to a physical device
- Bots running in an emulator may be harder to detect (no IMU delta)

Always test on physical devices for realistic accuracy numbers.

### What happens if two different people use the app on the same device?

BehaviorGuard stores one profile per installation. If multiple people use the same account on the same device, the second person's sessions will produce elevated risk scores (they'll look like an impostor to the first person's baseline). This is expected behaviour — BehaviorGuard is per-user, not per-account. For shared-device scenarios, either don't use BehaviorGuard or implement per-user profile management using the lower-level `BehaviorGuard` API with custom storage.

## Integration

### Do I need to forward every `MotionEvent` from `dispatchTouchEvent`?

Yes — including events you don't otherwise use. Pass-through events (finger hovering, tool changes, cancel events) provide useful context. The internal extractor filters what it needs; your code just needs to forward everything.

### What counts as one "session" for best results?

The richest sessions come from interactions that involve at least 20 keystrokes across a minute or more of engagement. A login form (username + password, ~15–25 characters) is borderline. A compose-message screen or a checkout form with multiple fields is ideal. Avoid using BehaviorGuard for single-field interactions or PIN entries — those have too few events.

### Should I call `startSession` / `endSession` for every screen, or only specific ones?

Focus on screens where you want anomaly detection. Calling it on every screen increases the signal volume but also increases the risk of short sessions that return `Error`. A good pattern is to gate session start/end on screens known to have enough interaction (forms, message composers, settings pages with multiple inputs).

### How do I handle `SessionOutcome.Error`?

Silently ignore it and do not penalise the user. Log it for debugging if needed. The user simply didn't provide enough signal in that interaction — that's not suspicious, just uninformative.
